So What? Executive summary + what changed since you last looked (vs. week of June 15)

The metals complex cooled in June after a strong spring, and the move is more about the macro backdrop than about anything structural. Copper, lithium and nickel each peaked in early-to-mid May and have since pulled back: copper to around $6.05/lb, near a seven-week low; lithium off its mid-May high of roughly CNY 200,000/t to the CNY 152,000–169,000 range; and nickel from above $19,600/t to about $17,200/t, a two-month low. The shared driver is a firmer US dollar and a more hawkish Federal Reserve under new chair Kevin Warsh, plus profit-taking, rather than a change in the long-run supply-demand picture.

That distinction is the whole point for the portfolio. The structural theses underneath both Nth Cycle and Aepnus — domestic refining capacity, the lithium-refining buildout, and the policy push to onshore critical materials — are intact even as the spot tape softens. For a company weighing a raise, the read is that spring strength has cooled but the multi-year case has not broken: this looks like a cyclical breather, not a regime change. The near-term effect on Nth Cycle's metal-linked economics is a modest, cyclical trim; Aepnus is largely insulated because its driver is refining capacity, not metal prices.

Both named catalysts moved this quarter, and neither in the direction the headline framing implies. There is still no US black-mass export ban — a potential Nth Cycle tailwind that has not arrived and is not expected imminently. And Rio Tinto has slowed construction at Nemaska's Bécancour plant, which is a timing risk for Aepnus's pilot rather than the clean positive the project's existence might suggest. Both are detailed below.

Nickel fell to about $17,200/t by June 24, a two-month low, after a rally above $19,600 in early May. Indonesia's 2026 ore-quota cut (to 250–260Mt from 379Mt) put a floor under sentiment, but a structural surplus and the industry's shift toward LFP chemistries — which use no nickel — cap the upside. Black-mass export ban
catalyst · not enacted
Worth stating plainly: there is no US black-mass export ban, and most market participants do not expect one imminently. The US is still a net black-mass exporter; the real constraint is a shortage of domestic refining capacity, not feedstock. For context, the EU is restricting black-mass exports from late 2026 and China (≈75% of global black-mass processing) reopened imports in 2025 to pull feedstock in. This is a potential future tailwind for Nth Cycle — restricted exports would keep recycled feedstock onshore — but it is not live policy. Treat as a standing alert, not a base case.

Nemaska / Bécancour
catalyst · slowed
The more consequential move this quarter. Rio Tinto took majority control of Nemaska (53.9%, via its Arcadium acquisition) and assumed management in February; on March 13 it announced it was slowing construction of the Bécancour lithium-hydroxide plant for an "optimization" period and temporarily cutting the contractor workforce, even though the plant is over 70% built. Portfolio Company Impact how the macro and the catalysts translate to each company

Nth Cycle
Domestic critical-mineral refiner
This week's read

June's pullback in nickel and lithium trims the near-term value of the Trafigura offtake, which is priced on the metals Nth Cycle produces (2,000 t/yr of contained nickel in MHP and 1,500 t/yr of lithium carbonate). The same volumes are worth somewhat less than they were in May — but the effect is modest and cyclical: contained nickel near $17,200/t and lithium carbonate in the low-$20,000s/t both sit well above the 2025 lows that made refining economics marginal, and a 10-year term smooths the swings. The more durable point runs the other way — margins improve with metal-price strength against a relatively fixed feedstock cost, so the structural copper and lithium case is a tailwind even while the spot tape cools.

Competitive context — clearing in their favor

Li-Cycle filed for bankruptcy in May 2025 after its $485M Rochester hub stalled on cost overruns and it could not meet the conditions to draw a $475M DOE loan; Glencore bought the assets for roughly $40M and folded them into Glencore Battery Recycling. The capital-intensive centralized model failing is close to a direct argument for the modular, lower-capex approach. The counterweight is Redwood Materials, still well-capitalized (over $2B raised privately) and having walked away from its own DOE loan — the strongest competitor in the category is thriving, not distressed. Net: the field is thinning at the weak end while the funded end stays strong, so the live risk sits in execution and ramp toward the 2028 expansions, not in technology or demand.

What to watch

Whether the metals pullback deepens or reverses (a sustained slide would pressure refined-output economics); the LFP-versus-nickel chemistry shift, a slow but genuine demand question for the nickel/cobalt side of the book; and the black-mass export-ban discussion — not policy today, but a meaningful feedstock tailwind if it ever became policy, which is why it earns a standing alert.

Aepnus Technology
Reagent recycling from sulfate waste · pre-commercial
This week's read

The relevant move for Aepnus isn't a price — it's the Nemaska/Bécancour slowdown. Nemaska is one of three named feedstock partners for the Bécancour pilot, so Rio Tinto slowing construction and cutting contractor headcount there introduces some risk to the pilot's cadence. The diligence question worth running down: does the pilot's sodium-sulfate supply depend on Nemaska's plant being operational, or on its construction-phase and partner streams? Vale and Ultium CAM remain as the other two partners, which diversifies the feedstock risk.

Caveat: Aepnus is private and pre-commercial, so this read-through is more inferential than Nth Cycle's. The Nemaska partnership is real and named, but the pilot's exact dependency on Nemaska's plant status isn't public — flagged as a question to confirm, not an established risk.
What the dashboard should track for them

The signal that matters for Aepnus is North American refining and cathode capacity coming online, not the spot price of any metal — more lines running means more sodium-sulfate feedstock to address. The one price that does move the value proposition is caustic soda (and sulfuric acid): it sets how much a customer saves by regenerating reagents on-site versus buying virgin. Right now US caustic soda is soft on chlor-alkali oversupply, a mild headwind to that half of the savings — though for remote sites the disposal-avoidance and freight half, which this doesn't touch, is usually the larger piece.

Competitive context

The real competition is the status quo it displaces — landfilling sulfate waste and buying virgin reagents from incumbent chlor-alkali producers (Olin, Westlake, Occidental, Dow). Direct analogs doing on-site electrochemical regeneration from sulfate waste are few, which is both the opportunity (little direct competition) and the risk (an unproven market, long industrial sales cycles, scale potentially years out). The question is less "who wins the category" than "does it materialize on a fundable timeline."

What to watch

North American refining and cathode capacity coming online (the demand driver); the Bécancour pilot's progress and whether the Nemaska slowdown touches it; caustic-soda and sulfuric-acid prices, currently soft; and above all any move from pilot toward the 500+ tonne-a-year demonstration plant, the real de-risking milestone.

Regulatory & Supply Chain Developments US focus · catalysts flagged for alerts

Rio Tinto · Mar 13, 2026Catalyst
Rio Tinto Slows Nemaska's Bécancour Hydroxide Plant for "Optimization"
After taking 53.9% majority control of Nemaska (via the Arcadium acquisition) and assuming management in February, Rio Tinto announced it would slow Bécancour construction through 2026 and temporarily reduce contractor headcount, despite the plant being over 70% built. It reaffirmed commitment and a $300M+ 2026 Quebec investment; commissioning was previously planned for 2026 with first production in 2028.
Why it matters: Nemaska is a named feedstock partner for Aepnus's co-located Bécancour pilot, so this is the quarter's most direct portfolio read-through — a timing risk to confirm, partly cushioned by Vale and Ultium CAM as alternate partners. ping if status changes
Policy · ongoingCatalyst
US Black-Mass Export Restrictions: Discussed, Not Enacted
The US still treats black mass as non-hazardous and remains a net exporter; market participants broadly do not expect a near-term ban. The EU is moving to restrict black-mass exports from late 2026, and China (≈75% of global black-mass processing) reopened imports in 2025 — so feedstock is being pulled toward Asia while US domestic refining capacity lags.
Why it matters: The single highest-leverage potential catalyst for Nth Cycle — a ban would keep recycled feedstock onshore — but it is not policy today and should be tracked, not assumed. ping if status changes
Industry · 2025–26Competition
Recycling Shakeout: Li-Cycle Bankrupt, Redwood Thriving
Li-Cycle's capital-intensive centralized hub model collapsed into bankruptcy in 2025 (Glencore bought the assets for ~$40M); Redwood Materials, by contrast, raised over $2B privately and declined its DOE loan. The category is bifurcating between distressed and well-funded players.
Why it matters: A real-world validation of Nth Cycle's modular, low-capex thesis — and a reminder that the strongest competitor (Redwood) is healthy, so the bet is on execution and capital discipline, not a clear field.
US Policy · FY2026Near-shoring
NDAA and FEOC Rules Keep Tightening the Domestic Floor
The FY2026 NDAA expanded covered materials and added explicit recycled-material exceptions, while FEOC rules continue to steer sourcing away from Chinese supply chains. The policy direction consistently rewards domestic refining and recycling.
Why it matters: A slow-moving structural tailwind under both portfolio companies' core thesis — onshore processing of critical materials — independent of where spot prices sit this month.
