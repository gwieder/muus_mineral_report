Executive Summary

Critical mineral markets are entering a period of acute structural tension. On the supply side, China's rare earth export control regime — expanded significantly in April and October 2025 and now covering the full processing stack — has created the most significant western supply chain disruption in a decade. The October 2025 measures were partially suspended until November 2026 following the Trump-Xi summit in Busan, but the underlying licensing architecture remains fully intact and can be reactivated at any time. On the demand side, the IEA's latest outlook projects copper demand up 30% by 2040, lithium up fivefold, and implied deficits of 30–40% for both by 2035 against currently announced project pipelines. Prices are responding: copper hit $6.01/lb on April 14 (up 30.6% YoY), lithium has staged a 78% recovery from December lows, and NdPr oxide is up 29% since December. The strategic implication is clear: supply concentration risk is no longer theoretical. It is happening now, and the window to reposition portfolios toward domestic and allied-nation supply chains is narrowing.

Strategic Analysis

Supply Risk The November 2026 Cliff — China's REE Suspension Expires in 5 Months

The most important date in critical minerals for the next 12 months is November 2026, now just five months away, when China's suspension of its most expansive rare earth export controls expires. The Oct 2025 measures — suspended following the Trump-Xi Busan summit — covered not just REE elements but the full processing stack: magnets, components, separation equipment, and recycling technology. If reinstated, they would represent the most comprehensive restriction on western access to rare earth-derived products ever implemented.

Meanwhile China is not waiting. The June 3rd appointment of CMRG to coordinate global mineral acquisitions shows Beijing is actively consolidating its position while the suspension window is open. The April 2025 controls remain in full force regardless, and the January 2026 catalogue expansion already added REE compounds to the control list.

For investors: the window between now and November 2026 is the period of maximum optionality and minimum time. Companies building non-Chinese REE supply chains — Lynas (Australia/Malaysia), MP Materials (California), USA Rare Earth (new South Carolina facility announced June 2) — are operating in a brief window where their product commands its strongest strategic premium. The DOE's $67M award to Colorado School of Mines for a rare earth processing plant signals the U.S. government is racing to close the gap before November. Either scenario after that date — controls reinstated or extended via deal — favors holding rather than exiting allied-nation REE assets.

Opportunity Processing Is the Value — and the Gap

The January 2026 U.S. Executive Order made explicit what market participants already knew: ore extraction without processing sovereignty is strategically worthless. China controls 40-90% of processing capacity for lithium, cobalt, and copper despite producing only ~10% of their ore. This is the chokepoint that matters, and it is where western investment is most insufficient.

The IEA projects real investment growth of just 2% in 2024 after adjusting for inflation — well below what's needed. The gap between demand projections and current project pipelines is 30% for copper and 40% for lithium by 2035. These are not tail risks. They are base-case outcomes under current investment trajectories.

The opportunity is concentrated in a specific part of the stack: domestic and allied-nation mineral processing, refining, and recycling. Redwood Materials (Nevada recycling at 95%+ recovery rates), Albemarle DLE (90% lithium recovery vs 50% conventional), and Lynas REE processing are the clearest examples of the category that policy, price, and supply dynamics are all pointing toward simultaneously.

Watch June 30 Copper Tariff Decision — The Most Market-Moving Date of the Month

On June 30, 2026, the U.S. Secretary of Commerce will deliver a recommendation to the White House on whether to impose 15% refined copper tariffs in 2027 and 30% in 2028. This single decision could fundamentally reshape global copper market structure for years. The anticipation of this decision has already triggered massive accumulation in U.S. domestic warehouses — nearly one million tonnes stockpiled — significantly tightening physical availability in European and Asian markets and widening the COMEX/LME price premium to historically unusual levels.

Copper hit a record $6.60/lb on June 2nd partly on tariff anticipation before pulling back to $6.28 today. Goldman Sachs raised its year-end 2026 copper forecast to $13,735/tonne. Jefferies now projects an average annual supply deficit of 491,000 tonnes through 2030. The structural case for copper is among the strongest of any commodity — AI data centers consume 3-10x more copper than traditional facilities, and the global grid buildout required for electrification is just beginning.

For the MUUS portfolio: any company with copper-intensive hardware (grid infrastructure, EV charging, solar installation) faces a meaningful cost inflection if tariffs are implemented. Conversely, companies with domestic U.S. copper sourcing or copper-light designs are positioned to benefit from the competitive moat those tariffs would create. The June 30 decision deserves direct attention from the portfolio management team this month.
